Most automation projects start too late. A workflow becomes painful, a spreadsheet becomes business-critical, and suddenly the team is relying on workarounds that were only meant to last a week.

The better approach is to look for early signals. If a task is repeated every week, depends on copying data between tools, or requires a person to remember several small steps, it is probably worth reviewing.

## Start with friction, not tools

Useful automation starts with the work itself. Before choosing software, map the current process and ask three questions:

- Where does information get entered more than once?
- Which tasks are delayed when one person is unavailable?
- What does the team avoid because it is tedious or easy to get wrong?

These answers usually reveal the highest-value opportunities faster than a broad software audit.

## Prioritise by business impact

Not every repetitive task deserves automation. Prioritise work that saves meaningful time, reduces customer friction, prevents expensive errors, or gives leaders better visibility.

The best first automation is usually narrow, measurable, and close to revenue or customer experience. Once that works, it becomes easier to build momentum across the rest of the business.

## Make adoption part of the build

Automation only works if the team trusts it. Keep the workflow visible, document what happens when something fails, and give people a simple way to review exceptions.

The goal is not to remove people from the process. The goal is to remove the low-value steps so people can spend more time on judgment, service, and growth.
